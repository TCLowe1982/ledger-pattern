# The Ledger Pattern

*The original paper, by TC. A proposed design pattern for applying Gang-of-Four
thinking in the age of the LLM. For the formalized spec see
[Ledger-pattern.md](Ledger-pattern.md); for the drop-in agent recipe see
[ledger-agent.md](ledger-agent.md).*

---

As computer science starts to explore the new paradigm of the LLM (Large Language Model), it is important not to forget the institutional knowledge of the past, and the lessons that they have taught us through hard won experience. The biggest, 'Don't reinvent the wheel,' and followed by, 'Don't repeat yourself.' Some may argue these are the same things, or different sides of the same coin, but that is beyond the scope of this investigation. My focus is on how we can apply the idea of the Gang of Four Design Patterns in the age of the AI. To that end, I am proposing a new Design Pattern, which I am referring to as the Ledger pattern.

One of the critical vulnerabilities with LLM is the lack of persistent memory. If only there was a device nearby that allowed you to record information on, which could easily be retrieved later in a more durable fashion. Sarcasm aside, LLMs are too often treated as 'do everything' machines, and end up doing everything poorly. The 'easy' solution that seems to be what the industry reaches for, 'more context' which solves some problems today, at massive engineering and capital costs.

A better way to deal with this problem is to use the system the LLM runs on, a computer, and the durable storage provided by writing to disk.[^1] The ledger pattern works by creating a temporary storage on the local disk, or in a designated cache location, creating a ledger of the parts, processing the parts one at a time, and then splicing them together at the end.

This allows a small local model to handle large inputs reliably with minimal data loss, and allows large models to operate more efficiently, and give higher quality answers to long, in depth questions. This pattern was discovered while building a long term memory unit for character based storage on an LLM frontend[^2] and trying to process long conversations and capturing the salient beats using local models. Specific model used was `Dolphin3:8b`[^3], but also tested with Claude Sonnet 4.6, Opus 4.6, 4.7, 4.8, and Fable, Grok, Gemma 4, Qwen 2.5, and 3.5, and others that have escaped note. The pattern has held across all instances, and increased processing depth for all models noted.

This pattern has two major use cases, when processing a large amount of text, or generating a large amount of text. I personally recommend partitioning your model context 1/6th for the actual prompt, 1/6th for the working chunk, and the remaining 2/3 for the response. This allows the model to have continuity of the original prompt throughout the process while iterating through the individual parts of the work. This holds for both use cases, large input and large output. For Large input, this is what keeps the model from being overwhelmed by the amount of text to be processed. For Large output, this reserves most of the space for the output content. And the general fractions allows larger context models to hold more, while not choking a smaller context local model.

One of the traps that large models will often fall into is taking a large amount of text into context, and losing fine details within that text. The example that helped discover this was a character beat about a one of the LLM Controlled characters deciding which character they would be. Various methods to capture this critical character moment failed, advanced sentiment analysis, fact extraction, time based. Eventually, I decided to break the scene up into smaller parts, and have it digest those individually, and then put the entire part together at the end, which allowed for the important character beats that got glossed over in the larger text. This was on a frontier model LLM, the smaller models couldn't process the large text. We repeated the experiment with the small pieces on a local model, and the results held.

When assembling the parts back together, the anti-pattern was also discovered. The principle of consensus-over-union was found to be essential. Union would take all the unique individual parts and paste them together, including the errors. This also allows one off errors to propagate down the line. This was solved by running the pieces through multiple times (at least 3) and selecting among the passes. In extraction that selection is consensus — keep the parts that most agreed with each other, and a one-off error drops out. The same 'run it three times' move is also what helps put together longer forms of content and maintain cohesion, though there the selection is best-of, not consensus (I separate the two below).

It's also important to note that multiple passes mean multiple chances for errors to occur. Slice overlap is another tool that points at cohesion for generation — carrying part of the adjacent window across the cut keeps the result reading smoothly as a whole. The caveat here is that normalization has to occur, otherwise the duplicated overlap will propagate downstream. This is crucial when choosing to use an overlapping method for tasks that require one, like writing a cohesive narrative.

It is easy to conflate the two different mechanisms that are at work here, and it's worth taking the time to explain them and their differences — and to note that each one forks by task type. The first is multi-pass: the LLM running the material at least three times. What you do with those passes depends on the task. For extraction it is consensus voting — keep the parts that are in agreement, which reduces single-beat variance and lets a one-off error fall out. For generation it is best-of selection — keep the most coherent rendering of the three rather than the most frequent fact. It is the same "run it N times" move; only the selection criterion changes, and best-of is a loose cousin of consensus. The other mechanism is slice overlap, which takes 10-20% of the adjacent window's text, and it forks the same way. For extraction the overlap is recall insurance: a fact sitting on or across a boundary isn't lost. For generation it is a continuity buffer: the narrative carries across the seam instead of lurching.

These two steps have an interaction, which is important to note. The overlapping fact now appears in two slices, and with a naïve consensus count it can falsely clear the bar. The fix for this is in the sequencing, normalize and collapse within pass duplicates first, then count agreement across passes. Without the normalization first, the overlap pass will ruin your consensus count.

The last major consideration is to run the items sequentially, and to notify the user of what is happening. Worst case scenario is sending all parts in parallel, and unless you are sending them to several different pieces of hardware, you are only going to be living in a world of pain, as you have just increased workload with no efficiency gain.[^4] Best case scenario, it takes noticeably longer, worst case scenario, you run out of ram and the entire process drags to a halt. And don't forget to inform the user of what is happening. If it just looks like the entire process stopped doing anything while it runs the Ledger pattern, it's going to look to them like a stall. Inform your users, and give them updates while the data is processing. I personally use a percentage system, updating when every piece gets sent to the ledger, and move on to the next part, but that is a UI decision. What matters is they know that something is happening, and actual work is taking place.

I know all of this sounds useful, but how do you actually incorporate this into your model's work routine?
